## Check-by-check results

**1. Deploy method honored the grant-preserve constraint — PASS.**
The doc's "Grant-safe deploy method" section explicitly avoids `bundle deploy` and uses a
code-only path: (2) `databricks bundle sync -t dev` (files only, "No resource reconciliation"),
(3) `databricks apps deploy geniefy-dev --source-code-path …` ("resource bindings untouched"),
then verifies `databricks apps get geniefy-dev` → `resources=[sql-warehouse, geniefy-db,
fmapi-endpoint]` preserved after deploy. Cross-checks confirm the churn risk the doc describes is
**real, not invented**:
- `databricks.yml` declares **only** `sql-warehouse` under the app's `resources:`
  (lines 88–93). `geniefy-db`/postgres and `fmapi-endpoint` are NOT declared as app resources
  (the `model_endpoint` at line 41 is a bundle *variable*, not an app resource binding; the
  comment at lines 82–87 explicitly states the `database` binding can't target Autoscaling
  Lakebase and is wired via `pg_host` + backend OAuth). So a `bundle deploy` would reconcile the
  app to `[sql-warehouse]` only and drop the UI-added bindings — exactly the doc's stated risk.
- `deploy.sh` step 4 is indeed `databricks bundle deploy -t … --var pg_host=…` (lines 121–124,
  "Deploy — databricks bundle deploy (App + geniefy_setup job + resource bindings)"). So avoiding
  step 4 was genuinely necessary to preserve the operator's grants.
- This matches the documented churn in the frozen `DEPLOY_VERIFY.md` finding #6 / operator-steps
  "Known fragility" (lines 28, 43) and U77/U78. The method is grant-safe by construction.
